Concept Overview

Simple Interest: I = Prt. Interest is linear. You only earn on the Principal.

Compound Interest: A = P(1 + i)n. Interest is exponential. You earn "interest on interest".

Head-to-Head Comparisons

Comparison 1: Investing (Making Money)

Scenario: You invest $10,000 at 5% for 20 years.

Simple Interest:
I = 10,000 × 0.05 × 20 = $10,000
Total Value: $10,000 (Principal) + $10,000 (Interest) = $20,000

Compound Interest:
A = 10,000(1.05)20 = $26,532.98

Difference: Compound Interest earned you an extra $6,532 for doing nothing differently.

Comparison 2: Debt (The Danger Zone)

Scenario: You owe $5,000 on a Credit Card at 20%. You don't pay it for 10 years.

Simple Interest (If it existed):
I = 5,000 × 0.20 × 10 = $10,000
Total Owed: $15,000

Compound Interest (Reality):
A = 5,000(1.20)10 = $30,958.68

Difference: Because of compounding, the real debt is DOUBLE what simple interest would be.

💸 Money Connection: The "Rule of 72"

Want to know when your debt doubles? Divide 72 by the interest rate.
At 20% interest (Credit Card), 72 ÷ 20 = 3.6 years. Your debt doubles every 3.6 years if you don't pay it!

Concept Overview

Total Cost of Borrowing (C.O.B.): The difference between the Cash Price and what you actually pay.

Formula: Total Paid = (Monthly Payment × Months) + Down Payment

4 Examples

Ex 1: The "Low" Monthly Payment Trap Car Price: $30,000. Loan: $500/month for 84 months (7 years).
Total Paid: 500 × 84 = $42,000.
Cost of Borrowing: $42,000 - $30,000 = $12,000 Extra!
Ex 2: Shorter Term = Less Interest Same Car ($30k). Loan: $800/month for 48 months (4 years).
Total Paid: 800 × 48 = $38,400.
Savings: You save $3,600 by paying it off faster.
Ex 3: Depreciation vs Payments By the time you finish paying off the 7-year loan (Ex 1), the $30,000 car might only be worth $10,000. You paid $42,000 for a $10,000 asset.
Ex 4: 0% Financing? Dealers sometimes offer 0% interest but increase the car price by $3,000. Always ask for the "Cash Price" to compare.
💸 Money Connection: The 1/10th Rule

Financial experts say your car expenses (gas, insurance, payment) should not exceed 10-15% of your monthly income. If you make $3000/month, your car budget is $300-$450 max.

Concept Overview

Credit Cards use Compound Interest calculated daily. The average rate is 19.99%.

4 Examples

Ex 1: How Interest Works Balance: $1000. Rate: 20%.
Annual Interest: $200. Monthly Interest: ~$16.66.
If you pay $0, next month your balance is $1016.66 (Interest becomes Principal).
Ex 2: The Minimum Payment Trap You owe $5,000 on a credit card. Min payment is $100.
If you only pay $100/month, it will take over 20 years to pay off and cost you thousands in interest.
Ex 3: The Grace Period If you pay your Full Statement Balance by the due date, interest is $0. This is the only smart way to use a credit card.
Ex 4: Cash Advances Taking cash out of an ATM with a credit card has NO grace period. Interest starts immediately, often at 22%+. Never do this.
💸 Money Connection: Credit Score

Your Credit Score (300-900) determines if you can buy a house or car. Missing a $20 credit card payment can ruin your score for years.

Objective: You will apply the financial concepts from this unit to make a realistic plan for purchasing and maintaining a vehicle based on a future career scenario, and demonstrate an understanding of credit card mechanics.

💼 Scenario

You have just graduated from college and landed your first full-time job. You need a car to get to work, but you must also prepare for unexpected life expenses using your knowledge of compound interest.

Part A: The Income (Inquiry)

1. Choose a career you are interested in (e.g., Electrician, Nurse, Graphic Designer).

2. Find the average entry-level annual salary for this job in Ontario. (Cite your source).

3. Calculate your estimated Monthly Net Income (Take-home pay). Calculate and deduct a realistic tax rate from your gross salary.

Part B: The Car Hunt (Inquiry & Application)

1. Go to AutoTrader.ca and find a used vehicle you would like to buy.

2. Record the Cash Price, Year, Make, and Model. (Include the link).

3. Use the Calculator Tab to calculate the Monthly Finance Payment.

  • Assume a Interest Rate of 7.99% (Standard used car rate).
  • Assume a Term of 60 Months (5 Years).
  • Assume 13% HST is added to the price.
Part C: The Monthly Burn (Application)

Owning a car is more than just the loan payment. Calculate your Total Monthly Car Budget:

  • Loan Payment: (From Part B)
  • Insurance Estimate: (Assume $250/month if you are under 25, or find a real quote).
  • Gas/Charging: (Assume $150/month for average driving).

Total Monthly Cost = Loan + Insurance + Gas

Part D: The Verdict (Communication)

The Rule: Financial experts warn that your Total Car Costs should not exceed 15% of your Monthly Net Income.

1. Calculate your percentage: (Total Monthly Cost ÷ Monthly Net Income) × 100.

2. Conclusion: Can you afford this car?

  • If Yes: Explain why this is a smart financial decision.
  • If No: What will you change? (Cheaper car? Longer term? Get a roommate to save money?). Write a specific plan.
Part E: The Credit Card Trap (Application & Communication)

A few months after buying your car, it requires an unexpected $1,500 repair. You do not have emergency savings, so you pay for the repair using a standard credit card with a 19.99% annual interest rate.

1. Calculate: Find the exact amount of interest the credit card company will charge you for the very first month.

2. Explain: Write a short paragraph explaining the mathematical danger of only paying the "Minimum Payment" every month. In your answer, clearly explain how Compound Interest works against you in this scenario, contrasting it with how Simple Interest would behave.
