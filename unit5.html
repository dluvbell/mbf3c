Unit 5 Lessons: Math & Money

Forget about pulling playing cards from a deck. We are going to learn how probability affects your wallet, your insurance, and your business.

Key Concepts

Theoretical Probability: What should happen in a perfect math world. (e.g., A coin should land Heads 50% of the time).

Experimental Probability: What actually happened in real life, based on data and history.

Example 1: Basketball Free Throws

Theoretically, an NBA player either makes the shot or misses it (50/50). But Experimentally, Steph Curry has made 9,000 out of 10,000 free throws in his career (90%). You would always bet your money on the Experimental data, not the Theory.

Example 2: Auto Insurance Reality

Theoretically, every driver should have an equal chance of crashing. But data shows 18-year-old drivers crash way more often than 40-year-olds. Insurance companies ONLY use Experimental Probability to set your prices.

Example 3: Fast Food Prizes (Monopoly Game)

McDonald's says you have a "1 in 4 chance to win!" (Theoretical). But if you buy 4 fries, you are NOT guaranteed to win. You might have to buy 20 fries before you win once (Experimental). The Law of Large Numbers says experimental probability only matches theoretical probability if you play millions of times.

Key Concepts

Probability compares Winners to the TOTAL. (e.g., 1 winning ticket out of 10 total = 10%).

Odds compares Winners directly to LOSERS. (e.g., 1 winning ticket : 9 losing tickets = 1:9 Odds).

Example 1: Business Startups

If a bank tells you the odds against a new restaurant succeeding are 4 to 1, that means for every 1 that succeeds, 4 fail. The probability of success is 20% (1 out of 5 total).

Example 2: Sports Betting Payouts

If the Raptors have "5 to 1" odds to win the championship, it means the bookies think they will lose 5 times for every 1 time they win. High odds mean low probability. If you bet $10 and they actually win, you get $50. You get paid more because the mathematical risk is huge.

Example 3: Video Game "Loot Boxes"

A video game says the probability of getting a Legendary skin is 1%. That means the Odds AGAINST you are 99 to 1. For every 1 person who gets it, 99 people wasted their money. Game companies use the word "probability" because 1% sounds better than "99 to 1 Odds."

Key Concepts

Independent Events: The past has absolutely zero effect on the future. The universe "resets" every time.

The Gambler's Fallacy: The foolish belief that a machine or a coin "remembers" what happened before, and that a certain result is "due."

Example 1: The Lottery Illusion

People buy tickets with numbers that "haven't come up in a while." Lottery machines have no memory. The probability of drawing a '7' is exactly the same today as it was yesterday. You cannot outsmart an Independent Event.

Example 2: The Casino Roulette Trap

You see a roulette wheel land on RED five times in a row. You bet your life savings on BLACK, thinking "Black is definitely due to hit!" Wrong. Spin #6 is completely independent. The chance of Black is still just ~47%.

Example 3: Flipping a Coin

If you flip a coin and get Heads 99 times in a row, the probability of getting Heads on flip #100 is still exactly 50%. The coin doesn't care about the past.

Key Concepts

Dependent Events: The first event permanently changes the conditions and probability of the second event.

Example 1: Credit Scores & Loans

If you miss a credit card payment, that is an event. When you apply for a car loan later, the bank views this as a Dependent Event. Your past action directly changed your "Risk Profile." Because you are now a higher risk, they increase your interest rate.

Example 2: Card Counting in Blackjack

If a dealer hands out four Aces in a row and doesn't put them back, the probability of you getting an Ace next is exactly 0%. The deck changed physically. This is called drawing "Without Replacement."

Example 3: Job Interviews

If 100 people apply for a job (1% chance), and the boss throws out 90 resumes, the 10 people left now have a 10% chance (1 in 10). The first event (throwing out resumes) radically changed the math for the second event (getting hired).

Key Concepts

Expected Value (EV): The mathematical average of what you will gain (or lose) if you repeat a financial decision thousands of times.

Formula: (Probability of Event × Financial Cost) = Expected Cost

Example 1: The Best Buy Warranty Trap

You buy a $1,000 laptop. The store offers a 2-year warranty for $200. The store knows from data that laptops break 10% of the time in the first two years.

Expected Cost of fixing it yourself: 10% of $1,000 = $100.

Since the warranty costs $200, the Expected Value for you is Negative (-$100). The store is mathematically guaranteed to make a massive profit off your fear over thousands of customers.

Example 2: The Casino Roulette Trap

A roulette wheel has 38 slots. Betting $10 on "Red" pays $10 if you win (18/38 chance) and loses $10 if you lose (20/38 chance).

EV = (18/38 × $10) - (20/38 × $10) = -$0.53.

Every time you spin, you mathematically bleed 53 cents. The casino doesn't need to cheat; the negative EV guarantees they make millions over time.

Example 3: Business Quality Control

A factory makes 10,000 pipes a month. Experimental probability shows 2% will be defective. If each pipe costs $50 to make:

Expected Loss = 2% of 10,000 pipes × $50 = $10,000.

The business MUST build this $10,000 expected loss into their monthly budget, or they will go bankrupt.

Key Concepts

If Expected Value proves that Warranties and Casino Games are mathematically designed to make you lose money, why is it considered "smart" to buy House Insurance or Life Insurance?

💸 The Rule of Catastrophic Impact

Yes, all insurance has a Negative Expected Value (you pay more in premiums than you get back in claims). However, we ignore the EV math here because of Impact.

1. House Fire Insurance

If a $1000 laptop breaks, it's annoying. If a $800,000 house burns down, you are instantly bankrupt for life. You accept a negative EV to transfer the risk of a "Catastrophic Impact" to a massive corporation.

2. Life Insurance (Income Replacement)

If you have three young children and you pass away, the loss of your income is catastrophic to their survival. Paying $50/month (Negative EV) is required to prevent total financial ruin for your dependents.

3. Auto Liability Insurance

If you cause a car crash and paralyze someone, the medical lawsuit could be $2 Million. No average person can afford this. Liability insurance is mandatory to protect society from catastrophic lawsuits.

Unit 5 Assignment: The Risk Analyst

Scenario: You are a Financial Risk Analyst. Your clients often make terrible decisions based on emotion rather than mathematics. You must use your knowledge of Expected Value, The Gambler's Fallacy, and Catastrophic Risk to give them cold, hard, mathematical advice.

💼 The Analysis Report

Read the three client scenarios below. Write a professional response for each one, using specific statistical concepts to justify your advice.

Part A: The Warranty Trap (Expected Value)

Your client is purchasing a $2,500 OLED Television. The salesman aggressively pressures them to buy a 3-year extended warranty for $400. You look up the manufacturer's data and find that this specific TV model has an 8% failure rate over a 3-year period.

  1. Calculation: Calculate the mathematical Expected Cost of the TV failing (Show your work: Probability × Cost).
  2. The Verdict: Compare the Expected Cost to the price of the warranty. Mathematically, how much profit is the store expecting to make off your client?
  3. The Advice: Write 2-3 sentences advising your client on whether to buy the warranty or keep the $400 in their bank account, using the term "Expected Value".
Part B: The Lottery Illusion (Independent Events)

Your client has been playing the exact same lottery numbers every week for 10 years. They tell you: "I've lost 520 times in a row. Statistically, my numbers are finally 'due' to hit! I am going to empty my savings account and buy 1,000 tickets this Friday!"

  1. The Diagnosis: What specific statistical bias/fallacy is your client suffering from?
  2. The Reality Check: Write a 3-4 sentence paragraph explaining why their logic is completely flawed. You must use the terms "Independent Events" and "Memory" to explain why past losses have absolutely zero effect on this Friday's draw.
Part C: The Insurance Paradox (Catastrophic Impact)

Your client recently bought a home. They ran the Expected Value math on their House Insurance and proudly declare: "The EV of my house burning down is only $2,000, but the insurance company wants to charge me $3,500 a year! It's a mathematically bad deal! I'm cancelling my policy today."

  1. The Pivot: Explain why strictly following Expected Value (EV) is a terrible idea when it comes to massive assets like homes or human lives.
  2. The Final Advice: Write 3-4 sentences using the concept of "Catastrophic Impact" to convince them why they must accept the Negative EV and keep the insurance policy active.
