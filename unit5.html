Unit 5 Lessons

Learn how to collect data, analyze it, and spot when people are using statistics to lie.

Concept Overview

You can't ask everyone in the world a question (that's a Census). Instead, you ask a small group (a Sample). How you pick that group determines if your data is good or garbage.

Types of Sampling

1. Simple Random Sampling Everyone has an equal chance of being picked. Like drawing names from a hat or using a random number generator. (Best method)
2. Systematic Sampling You pick every "n-th" person. Example: Standing at a door and asking every 10th customer who walks in.
3. Stratified Sampling You divide the population into groups (strata) first, then take a random sample from EACH group so it's proportional. Example: 10% freshmen, 10% sophomores, etc.
4. Convenience Sampling Just asking the people easiest to reach. Example: Asking your 5 best friends. (Terrible method, leads to heavily biased data).
💸 Money Connection: Market Research

If a company wants to launch a new product, they pay millions for good Stratified Sampling to see if people will buy it. If they use Convenience Sampling (just asking their own employees), they might manufacture millions of units of a product that real customers hate, bankrupting the company.

Concept Overview

These are three different ways to find the "middle" or "average" of your data.

The 3 Measures

1. Mean (The Average) Add all the numbers up, then divide by how many numbers there are.
Data: 2, 4, 6. Mean = (2+4+6)/3 = 4.
2. Median (The Middle) Put the numbers in order from smallest to largest, and pick the one exactly in the middle. If there are two in the middle, average them.
Data: 1, 3, 5, 9, 10. Median = 5.
3. Mode (The Most Popular) The number that shows up the most often. You can have no mode, or multiple modes.
Data: 2, 2, 4, 5, 5, 5, 8. Mode = 5.
💸 Money Connection: The "Average" Trap

Imagine 9 teachers earn $50k, and the principal earns $500k. The Mean salary is $95k. If you apply for a job there thinking you'll make $95k, you are being tricked by the Mean!

The Median salary is $50k. For money (like house prices or salaries), the Median is always the most honest number because it ignores crazy extremes (outliers).

Concept Overview

Central tendency tells us the middle, but Spread tells us how scattered or unpredictable the data is.

Key Concepts

1. Range The simplest measure of spread. Maximum Value minus Minimum Value.
Data: 10, 15, 20, 80. Range = 80 - 10 = 70.
2. Outliers A data point that is abnormally far away from all the other points. Outliers severely distort the Mean and the Range.
3. Standard Deviation (Concept) We don't need to manually calculate this in MBF3C, but conceptually: It measures how closely clumped the data is around the mean. A small standard deviation means everyone is very close to average. A large one means results are wildly scattered.
💸 Money Connection: Risk and Volatility

In the stock market, "Spread" is called Volatility (Risk). If Stock A returns between 4% and 6% every year (small range), it is low risk. If Stock B returns between -20% and +30% (huge range), it is high risk. Spread helps you decide where to put your retirement money.

Concept Overview

Bias happens when a survey is flawed in a way that artificially pushes the results in one direction.

3 Types of Bias

1. Sampling Bias Your sample doesn't represent the population. Example: Trying to find the most popular sport in Canada by only surveying people inside a hockey arena.
2. Non-Response Bias When a specific group of people refuse to answer your survey, ruining the data. Example: A restaurant emails a survey. Only extremely angry customers take the time to reply. The data looks like 100% of people hate the food.
3. Response Bias (Leading Questions) When the question itself forces people to answer a certain way. Example: "Do you agree that our heroic, hard-working mayor deserves a raise?"
💸 Money Connection: Fake "Studies"

Companies constantly use Response Bias to sell you things. They will run a survey saying, "Would you rather use our advanced cleaning product or stay dirty?" and then print "100% of people prefer our product!" on the box. Knowing statistics protects you from being manipulated.

Unit 5 Assignment: The Data Detective

Scenario: You are a financial analyst. People often use "averages" to trick consumers. Your job is to collect real-world data and prove why the Mean can be a massive lie, and why the Median is the truth.

💼 Your Mission Setup (Real Data)

1. Go to AutoTrader.ca or Realtor.ca.

2. Pick a specific item (e.g., 2018 Honda Civics, or 2-Bedroom Condos in Toronto).

3. Find the prices of 10 normal listings. Write them down.

4. Find 1 extreme Outlier (e.g., a heavily damaged car that is incredibly cheap, or a luxury penthouse that is massively expensive). Add this as your 11th number.

Question 1: The Calculations (Knowledge & Application)

Using your 11 real-world data points:

  • Mean: Calculate the Mean price of your items. Show your addition and division.
  • Median: Order the 11 items from cheapest to most expensive and identify the exact Median.
  • Range: Calculate the Range (Maximum - Minimum) of your 11 items.

Question 2: The Outlier Effect (Inquiry)

  • Explain: Look at your Mean and your Median. Which one is higher/lower? Explain mathematically exactly how the 1 extreme Outlier you chose managed to "pull" the Mean away from the real normal prices.

Question 3: The Sales Pitch (Communication)

Imagine you work for an advertising agency trying to sell these items.

  • Explain: Write a 3-sentence advertisement. Deliberately use the misleading measure (either the Mean or Median, depending on what makes your item look better to a buyer) to make the prices seem much cheaper or much higher than they actually are. Explain to me why you chose that specific number to trick the buyer.

Question 4: Spotting Bias (Inquiry & Communication)

A dealership sends out a survey to its customers saying: "Don't you agree that our premium car prices are perfectly fair given our world-class, award-winning service?"

  • Analyze: Identify the specific type of bias in this question (Sampling, Non-response, or Response bias).
  • Fix It: Rewrite the survey question so it is 100% neutral and will gather honest data.
