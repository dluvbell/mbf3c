Concept Overview

Exponents represent repeated multiplication. In bx, b is the base and x is the exponent.

5 Examples

Ex 1: Product Rule (Multiply) Rule: xa × xb = xa+b
Example: 23 × 24 = 27 (Add exponents)
Ex 2: Quotient Rule (Divide) Rule: xa ÷ xb = xa-b
Example: 56 ÷ 52 = 54 (Subtract exponents)
Ex 3: Power of a Power Rule: (xa)b = xa × b
Example: (32)4 = 38 (Multiply exponents)
Ex 4: Zero Exponent Rule: x0 = 1 (Always!)
Example: 1,000,0000 = 1
Ex 5: Negative Exponents Rule: x-a = 1 / xa (A negative exponent creates a fraction, NOT a negative number!)
Example: 2-3 = 1 / 23 = 1 / 8

Concept Overview

Growth where the rate of increase speeds up. It creates a "J" shaped curve.
Formula: y = a(b)x (where b > 1)

4 Examples

Ex 1: Bacteria Growth Starts with 100, doubles every hour (b=2).
Eq: y = 100(2)x
Ex 2: Inflation (Price Hike) A burger costs $5. Inflation is 10% (0.10) per year.
Growth factor b = 1 + 0.10 = 1.10.
Eq: y = 5(1.10)x
Ex 3: Real Estate A condo worth $500k goes up 5% per year.
b = 1.05.
Eq: y = 500,000(1.05)x
Ex 4: Viral Video Views start at 1,000 and triple (b=3) every hour.
Eq: y = 1,000(3)x
💸 Money Connection: Rule of 72

How long to double your money? Divide 72 by the interest rate.

  • Bank (2%): 72 ÷ 2 = 36 years to double.
  • Stock Market (10%): 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years to double.

Concept Overview

Formula: A = P(1 + i)n

  • A: Final Amount
  • P: Principal (Starting Money)
  • i: Interest rate (decimal)
  • n: Number of years/periods

5 Examples

Ex 1: Savings Account $1,000 at 5% for 3 years.
A = 1000(1.05)3 = $1,157.63
Ex 2: GIC Investment $5,000 locked for 10 years at 4%.
A = 5000(1.04)10 = $7,401.22
Ex 3: Credit Card Debt (Danger) $500 debt at 20% interest, ignored for 4 years.
A = 500(1.20)4 = $1,036.80 (Debt doubled!)
Ex 4: High Growth $2,000 in Tech ETF at 12% for 5 years.
A = 2000(1.12)5 = $3,524.68
Ex 5: Compounding Frequency Interest can be calculated Annually (1 time a year), Monthly (12 times a year), or Daily. The more frequently interest is calculated, the larger your final amount will be, because you start earning "interest on your interest" much sooner.
💸 Money Connection: Start Early

Compound Interest loves Time. Investing $100/month starting at age 20 creates way more wealth than starting at age 40, even if the 40-year-old invests more money later. The exponent n is the most powerful variable.

Concept Overview

When values drop by a percentage. The curve falls fast then flattens. It gets closer and closer to zero, but mathematically never actually touches it. This invisible floor is called an Asymptote.
Formula: y = a(b)x (where 0 < b < 1)

4 Examples

Ex 1: Car Depreciation A $30,000 car loses 20% per year.
You keep 80%, so b = 0.80.
Eq: y = 30000(0.80)x
Ex 2: Caffeine in Body 100mg coffee. Body removes 15% per hour (keeps 85%).
Eq: y = 100(0.85)x
Ex 3: Tournament Bracket 64 Teams. Half (50%) eliminated each round.
b = 0.5.
Eq: y = 64(0.5)x
Ex 4: Buying Power (Inflation) If inflation is 3%, your $100 bill loses value.
It is worth 97% of last year. b = 0.97.
Eq: y = 100(0.97)x
💸 Money Connection: The "New Car" Trap

Cars are depreciating assets. A new car loses ~15% value the moment you drive it off the lot. Smart financial planning involves buying used cars to let someone else pay for that steep initial decay.

Unit 3 Assignment: The Wealth Formula

Scenario: Instead of solving a generic textbook problem, you will apply the math to Your Own Life. You will research a real-world depreciating asset (a car) and compare it against the power of compounding investments.

💼 Your Mission Setup

1. Go to AutoTrader.ca and find a "Dream Car" you want to buy. Record its price ($P_{car}$): $________

2. Determine a realistic amount of money you could invest today ($P_{invest}$): $________

Question 1: The Depreciating Asset (3.4 Decay - Application & Knowledge)

Assume your dream car depreciates at a rate of 15% per year (meaning you keep 85% of its value).

  • Calculate: Write your decay equation y = P(0.85)x. Calculate what this car will be worth in exactly 5 years.
  • Evaluate: Subtract the future value from the original price. Exactly how much money "evaporated" (was lost to depreciation) over those 5 years?

Question 2: The Rule of 72 Reality Check (3.2 Growth - Inquiry)

Imagine you took that "evaporated" money from Question 1 and invested it in an index fund returning an average of 8% per year.

  • Calculate & Explain: Using the Rule of 72, how many years would it take for that lost money to double in the stock market? Show your calculation and explain what this means for your buying power.

Question 3: The Cost of Waiting (3.3 Compound Interest - Application)

Let's look at your starting investment ($P_{invest}$). Assume the market returns 10% per year (i = 0.10).

  • Scenario A (Start Now): Assume you are 17. Calculate the value when you turn 60 years old (n = 43 years) using A = P(1 + i)n.
  • Scenario B (Procrastinate): Assume you wait until you are 30 years old to invest that same amount. Calculate the value at age 60 (n = 30 years).
  • Evaluate: Subtract Scenario B from Scenario A to find your "Price of Procrastination."

Question 4: The Power of 'n' (3.1 Exponent Laws - Communication)

Look at your massive difference in Question 3. You only gave Scenario A an extra 13 years, but the final amount didn't just add a little bit—it exploded.

  • Explain: Using the rules of exponents (how multiplying bases adds the exponents, e.g., xa × xb = xa+b), explain to a friend why the variable 'n' causes money to grow in a "J-Curve" rather than a straight line.

Question 5: The Ultimate Financial Advice (Synthesis)

  • Explain: Write a short email to your 10-year-older self. Using your mathematical calculations from Q1 (the danger of decay) and Q3 (the magic of compound growth), advise yourself on how to balance buying depreciating assets (like cars) vs. investing early.
