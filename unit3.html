Concept Overview

Exponents represent repeated multiplication. In bx, b is the base and x is the exponent.

5 Examples

Ex 1: Product Rule (Multiply) Rule: xa × xb = xa+b
Example: 23 × 24 = 27 (Add exponents)
Ex 2: Quotient Rule (Divide) Rule: xa ÷ xb = xa-b
Example: 56 ÷ 52 = 54 (Subtract exponents)
Ex 3: Power of a Power Rule: (xa)b = xa × b
Example: (32)4 = 38 (Multiply exponents)
Ex 4: Zero Exponent Rule: x0 = 1 (Always!)
Example: 1,000,0000 = 1
Ex 5: Negative Exponents Rule: x-a = 1 / xa (A negative exponent creates a fraction, NOT a negative number!)
Example: 2-3 = 1 / 23 = 1 / 8

Concept Overview

Growth where the rate of increase speeds up. It creates a "J" shaped curve.
Formula: y = a(b)x (where b > 1)

4 Examples

Ex 1: Bacteria Growth Starts with 100, doubles every hour (b=2).
Eq: y = 100(2)x
Ex 2: Inflation (Price Hike) A burger costs $5. Inflation is 10% (0.10) per year.
Growth factor b = 1 + 0.10 = 1.10.
Eq: y = 5(1.10)x
Ex 3: Real Estate A condo worth $500k goes up 5% per year.
b = 1.05.
Eq: y = 500,000(1.05)x
Ex 4: Viral Video Views start at 1,000 and triple (b=3) every hour.
Eq: y = 1,000(3)x
💸 Money Connection: Rule of 72

How long to double your money? Divide 72 by the interest rate.

  • Bank (2%): 72 ÷ 2 = 36 years to double.
  • Stock Market (10%): 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years to double.
📊 Reading the J-Curve in Real Life (No Drawing Required)

When you look at a real exponential growth chart, here is what to notice:

  • Starting point (a): Where the line begins on the left — your initial investment or population at time zero.
  • The flat early phase: The first several years look boring. Nothing dramatic seems to be happening — but compounding is silently building a base.
  • The "elbow": The curve bends sharply upward. This is the moment accumulated interest starts generating more wealth than your original contributions ever did.
  • Real example: Search "S&P 500 historical chart" and zoom out to 1980–today. It looks nearly flat through the 1980s, then bends violently upward after 2000. That elbow is the J-Curve. The people who started investing in 1980 are the ones sitting on that vertical spike now.

Concept Overview

Formula: A = P(1 + i)n

  • A: Final Amount
  • P: Principal (Starting Money)
  • i: Interest rate (decimal)
  • n: Number of years/periods

5 Examples

Ex 1: Savings Account $1,000 at 5% for 3 years.
A = 1000(1.05)3 = $1,157.63
Ex 2: GIC Investment $5,000 locked for 10 years at 4%.
A = 5000(1.04)10 = $7,401.22
Ex 3: Credit Card Debt (Danger) $500 debt at 20% interest, ignored for 4 years.
A = 500(1.20)4 = $1,036.80 (Debt doubled!)
Ex 4: High Growth $2,000 in Tech ETF at 12% for 5 years.
A = 2000(1.12)5 = $3,524.68
Ex 5: Compounding Frequency — A = P(1 + r/n)nt When interest is calculated more than once per year, use the extended formula:
A = P(1 + r/n)nt — where n = compounding periods per year, t = total years.

Example: $2,000 at 6% annual rate for 5 years:
• Annually (n=1): A = 2000(1 + 0.06/1)1×5 = $2,676.45
• Monthly (n=12): A = 2000(1 + 0.06/12)12×5 = $2,697.70
Monthly earns $21.25 more — modest over 5 years, but the gap widens dramatically over decades.
💸 Money Connection: Start Early

Compound Interest loves Time. Investing $100/month starting at age 20 creates way more wealth than starting at age 40, even if the 40-year-old invests more money later. The exponent n is the most powerful variable.

📊 Reading the Compound Interest Curve (No Drawing Required)

When you look at a compound interest graph, here is what each part tells you:

  • Y-axis (left side): Dollar value of your account over time.
  • X-axis (bottom): Years elapsed since you started investing.
  • The slow start: Early years barely look like anything is happening. This is normal — the base is still small.
  • Where does the "Start Early" rule come from? The last 10 years of a 40-year investment produce more dollars than the first 30 years combined. Starting 5 years later means you miss the steepest, most explosive part of the curve entirely.

Concept Overview

When values drop by a percentage. The curve falls fast then flattens. It gets closer and closer to zero, but mathematically never actually touches it. This invisible floor is called an Asymptote.
Formula: y = a(b)x (where 0 < b < 1)

4 Examples

Ex 1: Car Depreciation A $30,000 car loses 20% per year.
You keep 80%, so b = 0.80.
Eq: y = 30000(0.80)x
Ex 2: Caffeine in Body 100mg coffee. Body removes 15% per hour (keeps 85%).
Eq: y = 100(0.85)x
Ex 3: Tournament Bracket 64 Teams. Half (50%) eliminated each round.
b = 0.5.
Eq: y = 64(0.5)x
Ex 4: Buying Power (Inflation) If inflation is 3%, your $100 bill loses value.
It is worth 97% of last year. b = 0.97.
Eq: y = 100(0.97)x
Ex 5: Half-Life — when b = 0.5 exactly Half-life is the time it takes for exactly half of something to decay. The base is always b = 0.5.
Formula: y = a(0.5)x where x = number of half-life periods elapsed.

Real examples:
• Medication: A 400mg dose with a 6-hour half-life → after 18 hours (3 periods): 400(0.5)3 = 50mg remaining. Doctors time your next dose before it drops too low.
• Caffeine: Half-life is roughly 5 hours. One cup of coffee = ~200mg. After 20 hours (4 periods): 200(0.5)4 = 12.5mg — that is why late-night coffee still affects your sleep.
💸 Money Connection: The "New Car" Trap

Cars are depreciating assets. A new car loses ~15% value the moment you drive it off the lot. Smart financial planning involves buying used cars to let someone else pay for that steep initial decay.

📊 Reading the Decay Curve in Real Life (No Drawing Required)

When you see an exponential decay chart — like a car's resale value over time — notice these three parts:

  • The peak (a): The starting value, far left. The sticker price or original dose.
  • The steep initial drop: Years 1–3 for a car are brutal. Most of the total value lost happens here. This is the "New Car Trap."
  • The flat tail (Asymptote): As the value gets smaller, each year's percentage loss is also smaller in dollar terms. A 15-year-old car still technically has a positive value — the curve never actually hits zero.
  • Real example: Look at any car's trade-in value chart on CarFax or Canadian Black Book. It drops sharply in year 1, flattens out by year 5, and barely moves after year 8. Smart buyers jump in at year 3–4 — after the steep part, before the car is worn out.
