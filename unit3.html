Unit 3 Lessons: Math & Money

Learn how the mathematical rules of exponents govern populations, viruses, and your bank account.

Concept Overview

Exponents represent repeated multiplication. In bx, b is the base and x is the exponent.

5 Examples

Ex 1: Product Rule (Multiply) Rule: xa × xb = xa+b
Example: 23 × 24 = 27 (Add exponents)
Ex 2: Quotient Rule (Divide) Rule: xa ÷ xb = xa-b
Example: 56 ÷ 52 = 54 (Subtract exponents)
Ex 3: Power of a Power Rule: (xa)b = xa × b
Example: (32)4 = 38 (Multiply exponents)
Ex 4: Zero Exponent Rule: x0 = 1 (Always!)
Example: 1,000,0000 = 1
Ex 5: Negative Exponents Rule: x-a = 1 / xa (A negative exponent creates a fraction, NOT a negative number!)
Example: 2-3 = 1 / 23 = 1 / 8
💸 Money Connection: The Penny Problem

Would you rather have $1 Million dollars today, or a penny that doubles every day for 30 days?

Day 1: $0.01
Day 2: $0.02
...
Day 30: 0.01 × 229 ≈ $5.4 Million!

Concept Overview

Growth where the rate of increase speeds up. It creates a "J" shaped curve.
Formula: y = a(b)x (where b > 1)

4 Examples

Ex 1: Bacteria Growth Starts with 100, doubles every hour (b=2).
Eq: y = 100(2)x
Ex 2: Inflation (Price Hike) A burger costs $5. Inflation is 10% (0.10) per year.
Growth factor b = 1 + 0.10 = 1.10.
Eq: y = 5(1.10)x
Ex 3: Real Estate A condo worth $500k goes up 5% per year.
b = 1.05.
Eq: y = 500,000(1.05)x
Ex 4: Viral Video Views start at 1,000 and triple (b=3) every hour.
Eq: y = 1,000(3)x
💸 Money Connection: Rule of 72

How long to double your money? Divide 72 by the interest rate.

  • Bank (2%): 72 ÷ 2 = 36 years to double.
  • Stock Market (10%): 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years to double.

Concept Overview

Formula: A = P(1 + i)n

  • A: Final Amount
  • P: Principal (Starting Money)
  • i: Interest rate (decimal)
  • n: Number of years/periods

5 Examples

Ex 1: Savings Account $1,000 at 5% for 3 years.
A = 1000(1.05)3 = $1,157.63
Ex 2: GIC Investment $5,000 locked for 10 years at 4%.
A = 5000(1.04)10 = $7,401.22
Ex 3: Credit Card Debt (Danger) $500 debt at 20% interest, ignored for 4 years.
A = 500(1.20)4 = $1,036.80 (Debt doubled!)
Ex 4: High Growth $2,000 in Tech ETF at 12% for 5 years.
A = 2000(1.12)5 = $3,524.68
Ex 5: Compounding Frequency Interest can be calculated Annually (1 time a year), Monthly (12 times a year), or Daily. The more frequently interest is calculated, the larger your final amount will be, because you start earning "interest on your interest" much sooner.
💸 Money Connection: Start Early

Compound Interest loves Time. Investing $100/month starting at age 20 creates way more wealth than starting at age 40, even if the 40-year-old invests more money later. The exponent n is the most powerful variable.

Concept Overview

When values drop by a percentage. The curve falls fast then flattens. It gets closer and closer to zero, but mathematically never actually touches it. This invisible floor is called an Asymptote.
Formula: y = a(b)x (where 0 < b < 1)

4 Examples

Ex 1: Car Depreciation A $30,000 car loses 20% per year.
You keep 80%, so b = 0.80.
Eq: y = 30000(0.80)x
Ex 2: Caffeine in Body 100mg coffee. Body removes 15% per hour (keeps 85%).
Eq: y = 100(0.85)x
Ex 3: Tournament Bracket 64 Teams. Half (50%) eliminated each round.
b = 0.5.
Eq: y = 64(0.5)x
Ex 4: Buying Power (Inflation) If inflation is 3%, your $100 bill loses value.
It is worth 97% of last year. b = 0.97.
Eq: y = 100(0.97)x
💸 Money Connection: The "New Car" Trap

Cars are depreciating assets. A new car loses ~15% value the moment you drive it off the lot. Smart financial planning involves buying used cars to let someone else pay for that steep initial decay.

Concept Overview

Simple Interest: Interest is linear. You only earn interest on the original Principal amount. It is a flat, steady line.

Compound Interest: Interest is exponential. You earn "interest on interest". It forms a J-Curve that accelerates upwards over time.

Knowledge & Application Examples

Ex 1 (Knowledge): Calculate the Basics

Problem: Use the Simple vs Compound Calculator to find the final amounts for $10,000 invested at 5% for 20 years.

Simple Output: $20,000 (It grew by exactly $500 every single year).

Compound Output: $26,532.98 (The growth amount increased every year because the pile got bigger).

Ex 2 (Knowledge): Identifying the Growth Pattern

Problem: How can you tell if an investment uses simple or compound interest just by looking at the yearly profit?

Solution: If the account earns exactly $100 every single year, it is Simple Interest (linear). If the account earns $100 the first year, $105 the next year, and $110 the next, it is Compound Interest (exponential).

Ex 3 (Application): The Real Dollar Difference (Debt)

Problem: You owe $5,000 on a credit card at 20% interest and don't pay it for 10 years. What is the difference in total debt between Simple and Compound calculation?

Simple (if it existed): You would owe $15,000 total.

Compound (reality): You owe $30,958.68.

The Difference: Because banks use compound interest on debt, you owe $15,958.68 MORE than you would with simple interest.

Ex 4 (Application): Long-Term vs Short-Term Impact

Problem: Compare investing $5,000 at 8% for 5 years versus 40 years using the calculator.

Solution: In the short term (5 years), the difference is small. But at 40 years, Simple Interest yields $21,000, while Compound Interest explodes to $108,622. Compound interest requires Time (n) to show its true power.

Inquiry & Communication Examples

Ex 5 (Inquiry): Visualizing the Curve

Problem: If you graph both on the same chart over 40 years, how do they physically look?

Analysis: Simple interest is a perfectly straight ramp. Compound interest starts out looking similar for the first few years, but eventually shoots vertically into a massive J-Curve, leaving the simple interest line far behind.

Ex 6 (Inquiry): Compounding Frequency

Problem: Why does a mortgage that compounds "Daily" cost you more than one that compounds "Annually"?

Analysis: Compounding daily means the bank calculates today's interest and adds it to your total debt immediately. Tomorrow, you are charged interest on a slightly larger debt. The faster the frequency, the faster the exponential curve grows.

Ex 7 (Communication): "Interest on Interest"

Problem: Explain to a friend why Compound Interest is better for retirement accounts than Simple Interest without using math formulas.

Explanation: "With Simple Interest, only your original money works for you. But with Compound Interest, the interest you earned last year gets added to the pile, and now that new, bigger pile earns even MORE interest this year. It's like your money is making babies, and those babies are making babies."

Ex 8 (Communication): Explaining to a Saver

Problem: A friend says they just keep their cash hidden in a drawer because it's "safe". Advise them.

Explanation: "Cash in a drawer actually loses value every year due to inflation (Exponential Decay). To protect your buying power, you must put it in an investment that uses Compound Growth to outpace the rate of inflation."

💸 Money Connection: The "Rule of 72"

Want to know when your compound debt (or investment) doubles? Divide 72 by the interest rate.
At 20% interest (Credit Card), 72 ÷ 20 = 3.6 years. Your debt doubles every 3.6 years if you don't pay it!

The Cost of Borrowing

Total Cost of Borrowing (C.O.B.): The difference between the Cash Price and what you actually pay over the life of the loan.

Calculation: Total Paid = (Monthly Payment × Months) + Down Payment

Financing vs Leasing

When you get a car, you don't just "buy" it. You choose how to pay for it.

Financing (Buying) Leasing (Renting)
You are taking a loan to buy the entire car. When the payments end, you own the car. You are paying only for the depreciation (the value the car loses) while you drive it for 2-4 years. Then you return it.
Pros:
  • You build equity (you own an asset you can sell later).
  • No mileage limits or wear-and-tear penalties.
Cons:
  • Higher monthly payments than leasing.
  • You are responsible for all repairs after the warranty ends.
 Pros:
  • Lower monthly payments.
  • Always driving a new car under warranty (no repair bills).
Cons:
  • You build zero equity. You rent the car and own nothing at the end.
  • Strict mileage limits (e.g., 20,000 km/year). Expensive penalties if you go over or scratch the car.

New vs Used Cars

New Cars Used Cars (e.g., 3-4 years old)
Pros:
  • Latest technology and safety features.
  • Full manufacturer warranty.
  • Often lower interest rates from the dealer.
Cons:
  • Massive Depreciation: Loses 15-20% of its value the second you drive it off the lot.
  • Higher insurance costs.
 Pros:
  • Someone else paid the depreciation: You buy the car after its biggest price drop.
  • Cheaper insurance and lower overall cost.
Cons:
  • Higher interest rates on used car loans.
  • Potential for expensive repair bills (no warranty).

Knowledge & Application Examples

Ex 1 (Knowledge): Total Amount Paid (Financing)

Problem: You finance a Honda Civic. You put $2,000 down and pay $400/month for 60 months. What is the Total Amount Paid?

Solution: Total Paid = ($400 × 60) + $2,000 = $24,000 + $2,000 = $26,000.

Ex 2 (Knowledge): Total Amount Paid (Leasing)

Problem: You lease a car for $350/month for 48 months with a $1,000 down payment. At the end, you return the car. Total Amount Paid?

Solution: Total Paid = ($350 × 48) + $1,000 = $16,800 + $1,000 = $17,800. (And you own nothing at the end).

Ex 3 (Application): Cost of Borrowing

Problem: The cash price of a car is $22,000. You finance it and your Total Amount Paid is $26,000. What is the Cost of Borrowing?

Solution: Cost of Borrowing = Total Paid - Cash Price = $26,000 - $22,000 = $4,000. This is the penalty for not paying cash.

Ex 4 (Application): Loan Term Comparison

Problem: A $30,000 car. Option A: 48 months @ $700/mo. Option B: 84 months @ $450/mo. Which costs more in the end?

Solution: Option A = $700 × 48 = $33,600. Option B = $450 × 84 = $37,800. Option B has a lower monthly payment, but costs $4,200 MORE overall!

Inquiry & Communication Examples

Ex 5 (Inquiry): The 0% Financing Illusion

Problem: A dealer offers 0% financing, but the car costs $35,000. If you pay cash, they give a "$4,000 cash discount" (price becomes $31,000). Is the loan truly 0% interest?

Analysis: No! The $4,000 difference is hidden interest baked directly into the finance price. You are paying $4,000 to "borrow" the money.

Ex 6 (Inquiry): Negative Equity

Problem: What does it mean to be "underwater" (have negative equity) on a car loan?

Analysis: Cars depreciate exponentially (steeply at first), while loan payments reduce the principal linearly (slowly). If you take a long 84-month loan, by year 3 your car might be worth $12,000, but you still owe the bank $18,000.

Ex 7 (Communication): The Salesman's Trap

Problem: Why do car salesmen always ask "What monthly payment are you looking for?" instead of "What is your total budget?"

Explanation: "By focusing on a low monthly payment, they can secretly stretch the loan term to 72 or 84 months. This maximizes the bank's interest profit and hides the massive Total Cost of Borrowing from the buyer."

Ex 8 (Communication): The 3-Year-Old Hack

Problem: Explain why buying a 3-year-old used car is considered a massive financial advantage over buying brand new.

Explanation: "Because of exponential decay, a new car loses the largest chunk of its value (up to 40%) in the first 3 years. By buying a 3-year-old car, the original owner has already paid for the most brutal part of the depreciation curve for you."

💸 The 15% Rule & Negative Equity

Your total car expenses (loan + gas + insurance) should not exceed 15% of your Net Monthly Income.

Concept Overview

Credit Cards use Compound Interest calculated daily. The average rate is 19.99%.

The Minimum Payment Trap You owe $5,000. Min payment is $100. If you only pay $100/month, almost all of it goes to exponential interest. It will take over 20 years to pay off.
The Grace Period If you pay your Full Statement Balance by the due date, interest is $0. This is the only smart way to use a credit card.
Cash Advances Taking cash out of an ATM with a credit card has NO grace period. Interest starts immediately, often at 22%+. Never do this.
