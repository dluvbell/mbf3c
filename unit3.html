Concept Overview

Exponents represent repeated multiplication. In bx, b is the base and x is the exponent.

5 Examples

Ex 1: Product Rule (Multiply) Rule: xa × xb = xa+b
Example: 23 × 24 = 27 (Add exponents)
Ex 2: Quotient Rule (Divide) Rule: xa ÷ xb = xa-b
Example: 56 ÷ 52 = 54 (Subtract exponents)
Ex 3: Power of a Power Rule: (xa)b = xa × b
Example: (32)4 = 38 (Multiply exponents)
Ex 4: Zero Exponent Rule: x0 = 1 (Always!)
Example: 1,000,0000 = 1
Ex 5: Negative Exponents Rule: x-a = 1 / xa (A negative exponent creates a fraction, NOT a negative number!)
Example: 2-3 = 1 / 23 = 1 / 8
💸 Money Connection: The Penny Problem

Would you rather have $1 Million dollars today, or a penny that doubles every day for 30 days?

Day 1: $0.01
Day 2: $0.02
...
Day 30: 0.01 × 229 ≈ $5.3 Million!

Concept Overview

Growth where the rate of increase speeds up. It creates a "J" shaped curve.
Formula: y = a(b)x (where b > 1)

4 Examples

Ex 1: Bacteria Growth Starts with 100, doubles every hour (b=2).
Eq: y = 100(2)x
Ex 2: Inflation (Price Hike) A burger costs $5. Inflation is 10% (0.10) per year.
Growth factor b = 1 + 0.10 = 1.10.
Eq: y = 5(1.10)x
Ex 3: Real Estate A condo worth $500k goes up 5% per year.
b = 1.05.
Eq: y = 500,000(1.05)x
Ex 4: Viral Video Views start at 1,000 and triple (b=3) every hour.
Eq: y = 1,000(3)x
💸 Money Connection: Rule of 72

How long to double your money? Divide 72 by the interest rate.

  • Bank (2%): 72 ÷ 2 = 36 years to double.
  • Stock Market (10%): 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years to double.
📊 Reading the J-Curve in Real Life (No Drawing Required)

When you look at a real exponential growth chart, here is what to notice:

  • Starting point (a): Where the line begins on the left — your initial investment or population at time zero.
  • The flat early phase: The first several years look boring. Nothing dramatic seems to be happening — but compounding is silently building a base.
  • The "elbow": The curve bends sharply upward. This is the moment accumulated interest starts generating more wealth than your original contributions ever did.
  • Real example: Search "S&P 500 historical chart" and zoom out to 1980–today. It looks nearly flat through the 1980s, then bends violently upward after 2000. That elbow is the J-Curve. The people who started investing in 1980 are the ones sitting on that vertical spike now.

Concept Overview

When you invest money, your earnings are added to your total — and then that larger total starts earning too. This is called compounding: interest on top of interest. The longer you leave it, the faster it grows.

🖥️ Using the Compound Interest Calculator (Calculator Tab)

Open the Compound Interest Calculator and fill in these fields:

  • Starting Principal: How much money you start with (e.g., $5,000)
  • Annual Interest Rate: Your investment's average yearly return (e.g., 8%)
  • Compound: How often interest is calculated — set to "Annually" unless told otherwise
  • Years: How long you leave the money invested

The calculator shows your final balance. To find the total interest earned, subtract your starting amount from the final balance.

4 Examples

Ex 1: Basic Savings You invest $1,000 at 5% per year for 3 years.
→ Enter into the calculator. Final balance: $1,157.63
→ Interest earned: $1,157.63 − $1,000 = $157.63
Ex 2: Long-Term Investing (The Power of Time) You invest $5,000 at 8% per year.
→ After 10 years: calculator shows $10,794.62
→ After 30 years: calculator shows $50,313.28
→ The extra 20 years turned $10K into $50K. That's the J-Curve.
Ex 3: The Price of Procrastination Two friends each invest $3,000 at 7% per year:
→ Alex starts at 18 → retires at 65 → calculator: $67,413.30
→ Jordan starts at 30 → retires at 65 → calculator: $29,804.71
→ Price of Procrastination = $67,413.30 − $29,804.71 = $37,608.59 lost by waiting 12 years.
Ex 4: Credit Card Debt (Compounding Against You) You carry a $500 credit card balance at 20% per year for 4 years.
→ Enter into calculator (it works for debt too). Final balance: $1,036.80
→ Your $500 debt nearly doubled just by being ignored.
💸 Money Connection: Start Early

Time is the most powerful variable. Investing $100/month starting at age 20 creates dramatically more wealth than starting at 40, even if the 40-year-old invests more money. The longer the money compounds, the steeper the J-Curve gets.

📊 Reading the Compound Interest Curve (No Drawing Required)
  • Y-axis (left): Dollar value of your account.
  • X-axis (bottom): Years since you started.
  • The slow start: Early years look flat — normal. The base is still small.
  • The "elbow": Eventually the curve bends sharply upward. The last 10 years of a 40-year investment produce more wealth than the first 30 years combined. Miss those final years by starting late, and you miss the most explosive part of the curve entirely.
Ex 5: Compounding Frequency (Annually vs Monthly) $2,000 at 6% annual rate for 5 years:
→ Compounded annually (once/year): $2,676.45
→ Compounded monthly (12×/year): $2,697.70
More frequent compounding = slightly higher total, because you earn "interest on interest" sooner in the year.

Concept Overview

Some things lose value over time — not by a fixed dollar amount, but by a percentage of whatever they're currently worth. This means the drop is huge at first, then gets smaller and smaller. It never fully hits zero — this invisible floor is called an Asymptote.

🖥️ Using the Depreciation Calculator (Calculator Tab)

Open the Depreciation Calculator and fill in these fields:

  • Asset Value / Cost: The original price of the item (e.g., $30,000)
  • Depreciation Rate: The percentage it loses per year (e.g., 20%)
  • Depreciation Method: Select "Declining Balance" — this models exponential decay
  • Years / Life: How many years you want to track

The calculator shows a year-by-year table. Read the value at your target year. To find how much was lost, subtract that year's value from the original price.

5 Examples

Ex 1: Car Depreciation A $30,000 car depreciates at 20% per year.
→ Depreciation Calculator shows Year 5 value: $9,830.40
→ Dollar lost: $30,000 − $9,830.40 = $20,169.60 evaporated in 5 years
Ex 2: Smartphone A $1,200 smartphone loses 35% of its value per year.
→ Year 3 value: $328.05
→ Dollar lost: $871.95 gone in 3 years
Ex 3: Inflation (Buying Power) Your $100 bill at 3% inflation per year.
→ After 10 years it only buys what $74.41 could buy today.
→ You lost $25.59 in buying power — even though the bill still says $100.
Ex 4: Half-Life (b = 0.5 exactly) A 400mg painkiller has a 6-hour half-life — every 6 hours, exactly half remains.
→ After 6 hrs: 200mg → After 12 hrs: 100mg → After 18 hrs: 50mg
→ Doctors time your next dose before levels drop too low.
Ex 5: The Smarter Buy Same $30,000 car, 20% depreciation per year:
→ Buy new: absorb $20,170 in losses over 5 years.
→ Buy at Year 3 for ~$19,200: absorb only ~$6,900 over 2 more years.
→ Same car. ~$13,000 in savings just by timing the purchase.
💸 Money Connection: The "New Car" Trap

A new car loses the most value in Years 1–3. Smart buyers let someone else absorb that steep drop by purchasing a 3-year-old used car — they get most of the car's life with far less of the depreciation loss.

📊 Reading the Decay Curve in Real Life (No Drawing Required)
  • The steep initial drop: Years 1–3 are brutal. Most total value is lost here — this is the "New Car Trap."
  • The flat tail (Asymptote): By year 5–8 the curve flattens. Each year's dollar loss gets smaller even though the percentage is the same.
  • Real check: Search any car on AutoTrader.ca and compare new price vs 3-year-old price vs 6-year-old price. You'll see the depreciation curve in real numbers.
