Unit 2 Lessons

Learn how curves (parabolas) model real-world trends like profit, gravity, and market cycles.

Concept Overview

Linear: A straight line trend. (Steady growth). y = mx + b
Quadratic: A curve (U-shape) called a Parabola. It goes up and comes down (or down then up). y = ax² + bx + c

8 Examples

Example 1: Hourly Wage (Linear) You earn $15 per hour. If you work more hours, your money goes up in a straight line at a constant rate.
Example 2: Throwing a Ball (Quadratic) You throw a ball in the air. It goes up, slows down, reaches a peak, and accelerates as it falls back down.
Example 3: Finite Differences (Quadratic) Look at the y-values: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25.
1st Diff: 3, 5, 7, 9 (Not constant).
2nd Diff: 2, 2, 2 (Constant). If the Second Difference is constant, it's Quadratic.
Example 4: The Equation y = x² - 4 is Quadratic because the highest exponent is ². (Unlike y = 2x + 5 which is Linear).
Example 5: Saving Money (Linear) Putting exactly $50 into a jar every single week forms a perfectly straight, linear line.
Example 6: Social Media Viral Hype (Quadratic) A new meme gains followers slowly, suddenly explodes to a massive peak, and then people lose interest and views drop off forming a parabola.
Example 7: Area of a Square (Quadratic) If you double the side length of a square garden, the area doesn't just double; it quadruples (Area = x²). This is quadratic growth.
Example 8: Car Depreciation (Not Quadratic) A car losing 20% of its value every year is actually Exponential, not Quadratic or Linear.
💸 Money Connection: The Hype Cycle & Impulse Buying

Fashion trends, memes, and viral products often follow a parabolic curve. They shoot up in popularity quickly to a peak (Vertex), and then crash just as fast.

Financial Lesson: Don't buy into the hype on the steep right side of the curve (FOMO). If you buy at the peak (Vertex), the value will only go down. Smart consumers wait for the trend to stabilize or buy before the curve goes vertical.

Concept Overview

Vertex: The turning point of the parabola. It is either the Maximum (Peak) or Minimum (Valley).

Vertex Form: y = a(x - h)² + k
The Vertex is at (h, k).

8 Examples

Example 1: Finding the Vertex (Minimum) Equation: y = 2(x - 3)² + 5
Solution: Vertex is (3, 5). This is the lowest point because 'a' is positive (2).
Example 2: A Negative Curve (Maximum) Equation: y = -1(x + 2)² + 10
Solution: h is -2 (sign switch!), k is 10. Vertex is (-2, 10). It's a Peak because 'a' is negative.
Example 3: Rocket Flight Interpretation A rocket's height is y = -5(t - 4)² + 100.
Solution: Vertex is (4, 100). At 4 seconds, the rocket hits its absolute max height of 100 meters.
Example 4: Profit Model Profit P = -20(x - 50)² + 5000.
Solution: The Vertex is (50, 5000). Max profit is $5000 when the price x is $50.
Example 5: Submarine Dive (Minimum) Depth d = 4(t - 10)² - 400.
Solution: The sub hits its lowest depth of -400 meters exactly at the 10-minute mark.
Example 6: Missing 'h' Value Equation: y = -3x² + 50.
Solution: Because there are no brackets, h is 0. Vertex is (0, 50).
Example 7: The "Sweet Spot" If a crop yield equation has a vertex at (20, 800), it means planting exactly 20 seeds per square meter yields the maximum 800 crops.
Example 8: Missing 'k' Value Equation: y = 5(x - 7)².
Solution: There is no number at the end, so k is 0. Vertex is (7, 0).
💸 Money Connection: Speeding vs. Gas Money

Did you know air resistance increases with the square of your speed ($v^2$)? Driving at 120km/h uses significantly more gas than 100km/h for a small time gain.

Financial Lesson: Every car has an "Optimal Speed" (Vertex) for fuel efficiency, usually around 90-100km/h. Driving faster than this vertex literally burns money out of your tailpipe. Finding the vertex saves your wallet.

Concept Overview

Standard Form: y = ax² + bx + c.
y-intercept: The point where the graph crosses the y-axis (where x=0). In standard form, the y-intercept is c.

8 Examples

Example 1: Expanding (FOIL) Convert y = (x + 2)(x + 3) to standard form.
Solution: x² + 3x + 2x + 6 = x² + 5x + 6.
Example 2: Finding y-intercept Equation: y = 2x² - 5x + 12.
Solution: Let x = 0. y = 12. The y-intercept is 12.
Example 3: Business Startup Cost A business profit model is y = -x² + 10x - 50.
Solution: The y-intercept is -50. At Day 0 (start), the business has a loss of $50 (Setup cost).
Example 4: Direction of Opening y = -3x² + 4x + 1.
Solution: Since 'a' is negative (-3), the parabola opens DOWN (frown).
Example 5: Throwing off a Cliff Height equation: h = -5t² + 20t + 45.
Solution: The y-intercept is 45. This means the person was standing on a 45m cliff when they threw the ball at t=0.
Example 6: Finding x-intercepts (Break-Even) If Profit P = -2(x - 10)(x - 40), the x-intercepts are 10 and 40. The business breaks even if they charge $10 or $40.
Example 7: The Missing 'c' Equation: y = 4x² - 8x.
Solution: There is no constant term, so c = 0. The graph starts exactly at the origin (0,0).
Example 8: Standard Form from Vertex Form y = (x - 3)² + 4 → (x - 3)(x - 3) + 4 → x² - 6x + 9 + 4 → y = x² - 6x + 13.
💸 Money Connection: Side Hustle Startup Costs

In a business quadratic model ($y = ax^2 + bx + c$), the constant 'c' represents your Startup Cost (Initial Investment). It is usually a negative number in your bank account.

Financial Lesson: Whether it's buying a lawnmower or art supplies, you start in debt ($c$). You must work hard to cross the "Break-even point" (x-intercept) before you actually start making profit. Watch your 'c' value!

Concept Overview

In business, you can't just raise prices forever. If price is too high, no one buys. If too low, you make no money.

The relationship between Price and Profit is often Quadratic. The Vertex is the "Sweet Spot".

8 Examples

Example 1: The Concept Low Price = High Sales but Low Margin.
High Price = High Margin but Low Sales.
Middle Price = Maximum Profit (Vertex).
Example 2: Break-Even Points (x-intercepts) The x-intercepts (where Profit = $0) are the break-even points. You must price your product between these two points to make any money.
Example 3: Substitution (The Greed Scenario) Profit P = -5(x - 20)² + 1000. (Max profit is $1000 at $20).
What if you get greedy and charge $30?
Solution: Substitute x = 30. P = -5(30 - 20)² + 1000 → -5(100) + 1000 = $500.
Result: You raised the price, but total profit dropped!
Example 4: Market Risk (The 'a' value) If a competitor enters, consumers become price-sensitive. Your 'a' value changes from -2 to -10.
Meaning: The parabola becomes extremely narrow. The break-even points get closer together. If your price misses the sweet spot by a little, profits crash.
Example 5: The "Safe Window" If break-even points are $5 and $55, your safe pricing window is huge ($50 wide). Low risk market.
Example 6: Positive 'a' in Business? If a business model had a positive 'a' value (opening up), it would mean they have a minimum profit (valley) and infinite maximum profit. This is physically impossible in real economies.
Example 7: The Panic Drop Sweet spot is $40. You panic and drop the price to $10 to sell more units. Profit drops dramatically because your margin per item is completely destroyed.
Example 8: Finding the Vertex from Intercepts If break even points are $10 and $30, the perfect price (vertex x-coordinate) is exactly in the middle: (10+30)/2 = $20.
💸 Money Connection: The Vertex of Happiness

Economists call this "Diminishing Returns." The joy you get from spending money follows a downward parabola. The first coffee gives you energy and joy. The 5th coffee just gives you a stomach ache.

Financial Lesson: Spending more doesn't always equal more happiness. There is a "Vertex" of satisfaction. The key to wealth is knowing when you've reached that peak and stopping there.

Question 1: The Model & The Peak (Vertex Form)

P = -2(x - ____ )² + ____

Question 2: Startup Costs (Standard Form & y-intercept)

Question 3: Breaking Even (x-intercepts)

A profit parabola typically has two x-intercepts (points where your profit is exactly $0 or your "break-even" points). One happens when your price is too low, and the other happens when your price is too high.

  • a) Explain what those numbers are for your model? (Write 1-2 sentences and include your reasoning.)
