Unit 2 Lessons

Learn how curves (parabolas) model real-world trends like profit, gravity, and market cycles.

Concept Overview

Linear: A straight line trend. (Steady growth). y = mx + b
Quadratic: A curve (U-shape) called a Parabola. It goes up and comes down (or down then up). y = ax² + bx + c

4 Examples

Example 1: Hourly Wage (Linear) You earn $15 per hour. If you work more hours, your money goes up in a straight line.
This is NOT Quadratic.
Example 2: Throwing a Ball (Quadratic) You throw a ball in the air. It goes up, slows down, reaches a peak, and falls back down.
The path is a Parabola.
Example 3: Finite Differences Look at the y-values: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25.
1st Diff: 3, 5, 7, 9 (Not constant).
2nd Diff: 2, 2, 2 (Constant).
If the Second Difference is constant, it's Quadratic.
Example 4: The Equation Which is quadratic?
A) y = 2x + 5 (Linear)
B) y = x² - 4 (Quadratic because of the exponent ²)
💸 Money Connection: The Hype Cycle & Impulse Buying

Fashion trends, memes, and viral products often follow a parabolic curve. They shoot up in popularity quickly to a peak (Vertex), and then crash just as fast.

Financial Lesson: Don't buy into the hype on the steep right side of the curve (FOMO). If you buy at the peak (Vertex), the value will only go down. Smart consumers wait for the trend to stabilize or buy before the curve goes vertical.

Concept Overview

Vertex: The turning point of the parabola. It is either the Maximum (Peak) or Minimum (Valley).

Vertex Form: y = a(x - h)² + k
The Vertex is at (h, k).

4 Examples

Example 1: Finding the Vertex Equation: y = 2(x - 3)² + 5
Solution: Vertex is (3, 5). This is the lowest point (Minimum) because 'a' is positive.
Example 2: A Negative Curve Equation: y = -1(x + 2)² + 10
Solution: h is -2 (sign switch!), k is 10.
Vertex is (-2, 10). This is the highest point (Maximum) because 'a' is negative (-1).
Example 3: Interpretation A rocket's height is y = -5(t - 4)² + 100.
Solution: Vertex is (4, 100). This means at 4 seconds, the rocket reaches its max height of 100 meters.
Example 4: Profit Model Profit P = -20(x - 50)² + 5000.
Solution: The Vertex is (50, 5000). Max profit is $5000 when the price x is $50.
💸 Money Connection: Speeding vs. Gas Money

Did you know air resistance increases with the square of your speed ($v^2$)? Driving at 120km/h uses significantly more gas than 100km/h for a small time gain.

Financial Lesson: Every car has an "Optimal Speed" (Vertex) for fuel efficiency, usually around 90-100km/h. Driving faster than this vertex literally burns money out of your tailpipe. Finding the vertex saves your wallet.

Concept Overview

Standard Form: y = ax² + bx + c.
y-intercept: The point where the graph crosses the y-axis (where x=0). In standard form, the y-intercept is c.

4 Examples

Example 1: Expanding (FOIL) Convert y = (x + 2)(x + 3) to standard form.
Solution: x² + 3x + 2x + 6 = x² + 5x + 6.
Example 2: Finding y-intercept Equation: y = 2x² - 5x + 12.
Solution: Let x = 0. y = 12. The y-intercept is 12.
Example 3: Business Start A business profit model is y = -x² + 10x - 50.
Solution: The y-intercept is -50. This means at day 0 (start), the business has a loss of $50 (Setup cost).
Example 4: Direction y = -3x² + 4x + 1.
Solution: Since 'a' is negative (-3), the parabola opens DOWN (frown).
💸 Money Connection: Side Hustle Startup Costs

In a business quadratic model ($y = ax^2 + bx + c$), the constant 'c' represents your Startup Cost (Initial Investment). It is usually a negative number in your bank account.

Financial Lesson: Whether it's buying a lawnmower or art supplies, you start in debt ($c$). You must work hard to cross the "Break-even point" (x-intercept) before you actually start making profit. Watch your 'c' value!

Concept Overview

In business, you can't just raise prices forever. If price is too high, no one buys. If too low, you make no money.

The relationship between Price and Profit is often Quadratic. The Vertex is the "Sweet Spot".

4 Examples

Example 1: The Concept Low Price = High Sales but Low Margin.
High Price = High Margin but Low Sales.
Middle Price = Maximum Profit (Vertex).
Example 2: Break-Even Points (x-intercepts) The x-intercepts (where Profit = $0) are the break-even points. You must price your product between these two points to make any money.
Example 3: Substitution (The Greed Scenario) Profit P = -5(x - 20)² + 1000. (Max profit is $1000 at $20).
What if you get greedy and charge $30?
Solution: Substitute x = 30.
P = -5(30 - 20)² + 1000 → -5(100) + 1000 = $500.
Result: You raised the price, but total profit dropped!
Example 4: Market Risk (The 'a' value) If a competitor enters, consumers become price-sensitive. Your 'a' value changes from -2 to -10.
Meaning: The parabola becomes extremely narrow. The break-even points get much closer together. If your price misses the sweet spot by just a little, profits crash.
💸 Money Connection: The Vertex of Happiness

Economists call this "Diminishing Returns." The joy you get from spending money follows a downward parabola. The first coffee gives you energy and joy. The 5th coffee just gives you a stomach ache.

Financial Lesson: Spending more doesn't always equal more happiness. There is a "Vertex" of satisfaction. The key to wealth is knowing when you've reached that peak and stopping there.

Unit 2 Assignment: E-Commerce Brand Launch

Scenario: You are launching a new e-commerce brand. Choose an item to sell (e.g., custom t-shirts, phone cases, sneakers). Your goal is not just to sell a lot of items, but to find the "Sweet Spot" (perfect price) to generate the Maximum Profit.

💼 Your Business Setup (Fill in the blanks)

1. The item I am selling: ____________________

2. My ideal Selling Price for this item (h): $________

3. The Maximum Profit I expect at that price (k): $________

*Note: For these calculations, we will lock the 'a' value (the steepness of the curve) at -2.

Question 1: The Model & The Peak (Vertex Form)

In business mathematics, the Vertex represents your "Peak Profit". Substitute your chosen h and k values into the equation below to create your personal profit model.

P = -2(x - ____ )² + ____

  • Explain: What are the coordinates of your Vertex (h, k)? What does this specific point mean for your business in the real world?
  • Hint: Use business terms. Explain that selling your item for $___ will result in making the most money possible: $___.

Question 2: Startup Costs (Standard Form & y-intercept)

Before you even sell a single item, you have to spend money (website hosting, manufacturing, marketing). Let's find out your initial costs by expanding your Vertex Form equation from Question 1 into Standard Form (P = ax² + bx + c).

  • Calculate: Expand your equation. (Remember: FOIL the $(x - h)(x - h)$ first, then multiply everything by -2, and finally add your $k$ value).
  • Explain: What is your y-intercept ('c' value)? Explain why your profit (P) is a negative number when the product price (x) is $0, connecting it to the concept of "Startup Costs".

Question 3: Breaking Even (x-intercepts)

A profit parabola typically has two x-intercepts (points where your profit is exactly $0 or your "break-even" points). One happens when your price is too low, and the other happens when your price is too high.

  • Explain: Using logic and consumer psychology, explain why pricing your item too high would cause your total profit to drop to $0 (or go negative).
  • Hint: Even if your profit margin per item is huge, what happens to customer demand (the number of people buying) if the price is outrageous?

Question 4: The "Greed" Scenario (Application)

Imagine you get greedy and decide to sell your product for $30 more than your ideal price (h).

  • Calculate: Substitute this new, expensive price into the x variable of your equation from Question 1 and calculate your new Profit (P).
  • Explain: Write a short email to your investors. Show them the math result, and explain why increasing the price per item actually caused the company to lose money overall.

Question 5: Market Shifts & Risk (Inquiry)

A massive competitor enters the market, making consumers hyper-aware of prices. Mathematically, this changes your 'a' value from -2 to -10.

  • Explain: Changing 'a' to -10 makes the parabola much narrower and steeper. Without doing any math, deduce what this visual change means for your pricing strategy's Risk.
  • Hint: If the curve is extremely narrow, what happens to your profit if your price misses the "Sweet Spot" (Vertex) by just a few dollars?
